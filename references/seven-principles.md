# The Seven Principles of Influence — Reference Card

> Source: Robert Cialdini, _Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion_ (William Morrow, 1984; expanded edition added the 7th principle Unity in 2016 alongside _Pre-Suasion_). Frameworks cited descriptively under nominative fair use.

## The seven

| # | Principle | One-line definition |
| - | --------- | ------------------- |
| 1 | Reciprocity | People are obliged to give back to others the form of behavior, gift, or service they have received first. |
| 2 | Commitment / Consistency | People like to be consistent with the things they have previously said or done. |
| 3 | Social Proof | When uncertain, people look to the actions and behaviors of others to determine their own. |
| 4 | Authority | People follow the lead of credible, knowledgeable experts. |
| 5 | Liking | People prefer to say yes to those they like. |
| 6 | Scarcity | People want more of what they can have less of. |
| 7 | Unity | The more we identify ourselves with others, the more we are influenced by them. (Added 2016.) |

Cialdini's umbrella term for these as cognitive shortcuts: **"weapons of influence"** (1984).

## Per-principle audit detail

### 1. Reciprocity

**Mechanic:** giving creates a felt obligation to give back. Works even when the gift is unrequested or small. Strongest when the gift is personalized, unexpected, and meaningful.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- Free useful insight given before the ask (specific observation, calculation, mini-audit, custom asset)
- A small favor done for the reader before the request appears
- The gift is named and concrete, not "a free PDF"

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- "Free trial" framed as the gift but experienced as friction (signup, credit card upfront)
- Generic lead magnet ("download our guide") not tailored to the reader
- The gift arrives AFTER the ask, not before

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- Copy opens with the request and never gives anything first
- Cold outreach with no value-add before the calendar link

**Common audit copy:** "Reciprocity is missing. The email asks for time without offering anything first. Add a specific observation about the prospect's [page / pricing / funnel] in the first 2 sentences."

### 2. Commitment / Consistency

**Mechanic:** small commitments build into larger ones. Once someone has said yes to A, they want to be consistent with that yes when asked about B (related to A). The reader's prior identity, choices, and stated values are commitment anchors.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- The copy invites a small commitment first (opt-in, click, micro-yes) that aligns with the larger ask
- The copy references something the reader has previously said, done, or believes
- The CTA path includes earlier yeses before the big ask

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- A multi-step funnel exists but the steps don't build on each other thematically
- Reader is asked to commit to a value statement that is too generic to mean yes or no

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- One-shot ask for the big yes (purchase, demo, signup) with no prior commitment ladder
- No reference to anything the reader has already said or done

### 3. Social Proof

**Mechanic:** uncertainty drives people to look at what similar others are doing. Strongest when the proof comes from people the reader perceives as similar to themselves, in recent and specific contexts.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- Specific named customers, customer counts (with verification), case studies with named results
- Testimonials from people who match the target audience (B2B testimonials on a B2B page; D2C on D2C)
- Recent (current year), verifiable evidence

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- Generic "trusted by thousands" without specifics
- Logos with no story attached
- Testimonials from people who don't match the audience

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- No third-party evidence anywhere in the copy
- All claims come from the seller's own voice

**Audit warning:** if you suggest a fix that adds Social Proof, NEVER fabricate the customer count, case study, or testimonial. Recommend the user supply real numbers.

### 4. Authority

**Mechanic:** people defer to credible experts. Authority is signaled through credentials, titles, named affiliations, third-party recognition, citations, and named work.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- The sender / brand has a named title and relevant experience
- Citations to recognized sources, journals, or third-party validators
- Named past work that matches the audience's reference set

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- Borrowed authority that doesn't match the audience (a Forbes mention to a developer audience that doesn't read Forbes)
- "Expert" claim with no specifics
- Credentials present but buried below the fold

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- Anonymous or first-name-only sender on B2B copy
- No credentials, no citations, no named work

### 5. Liking

**Mechanic:** people say yes to those they like. Driven by similarity, compliments (genuine), cooperation toward a shared goal, and physical / personal attractiveness signals (in person; in copy: voice and warmth).

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- Copy has a human voice — first person, named writer, perspective visible
- Reader's situation is acknowledged in a way that feels seen (not generic empathy)
- Tone is warm without being sycophantic

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- Corporate-voice ("we strive to deliver") with no human present
- Sycophantic openers ("I love what you're building!" without specifics)
- Faux-personalization ("[First Name]") that signals templated outreach

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- Cold, transactional tone with no warmth
- Reader is addressed as a market, not a person

### 6. Scarcity

**Mechanic:** people value what they can have less of. Strongest when the scarcity is real, named, and tied to a specific reason.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- Limited supply with a specific number (10 seats, 100 units)
- Limited time with a specific date and a real reason for the deadline
- Limited eligibility (only first-time buyers, only certain industries)

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- Vague urgency ("limited time only") without a specific deadline
- Scarcity present but the reason is unstated or implausible

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- No constraint on supply, time, or eligibility named anywhere
- Offer is open-ended

**Audit warning:** fake scarcity (countdown timers that reset, "only 3 left" that never depletes) is FRAUDULENT and destroys trust. If you detect it, score WEAK and flag it explicitly.

### 7. Unity

**Mechanic:** shared identity drives influence more strongly than mere liking. Unity is "we are the same kind of people" — it operates through in-group definition (shared geography, profession, struggle, values, identity markers).

**Distinction from Liking:** Liking is "I like you." Unity is "we are us." Liking can come from one warm interaction; Unity requires identity-level overlap.

**Audit signals (PRESENT):**

- Copy defines an in-group the reader belongs to (named profession, struggle, identity)
- "We" is used in a way that includes the reader (not the brand's "we")
- Shared values, history, or geography is named

**Audit signals (WEAK):**

- Friendly tone that signals Liking but stops short of Unity
- "We" used to refer to the brand only

**Audit signals (MISSING):**

- No in-group definition; reader is addressed as an outsider being marketed to
- Generic "for everyone" framing

**Audit warning (COMMON ERROR):** do not score Unity high just because the tone is warm. A friendly cold email is Liking, not Unity. Unity requires identity definition.

## Cross-principle interaction notes

- **Reciprocity + Commitment** stack well in nurture sequences (give first, then ask for a small yes, then build).
- **Authority + Social Proof** stack well in B2B (credentials + named customers).
- **Liking + Unity** are confusable; audit them separately.
- **Scarcity** without **Authority** can read as manipulation. Always pair scarcity with credibility.
- **Anti-stacking rule:** see `audit-rubric.md` for the anti-overstacking check.
